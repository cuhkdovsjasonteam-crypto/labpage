Workshop for SII: Spectrum Sensing and Sensing Driven Networking

Friday, December 18, 2020 -- 8 am PT to 12 noon PT

Dinesh Bharadia, Suman Banerjee

Goal: Sensing driven communication, Meeting the needs to co-existence, and building efficient networks

Problem statement: The need for spectrum is higher today than ever before. As user demands for wireless capacity grow, it has commensurately become necessary to better utilize such spectrum whenever and wherever possible. In recent times, FCC has opened up a variety of frequencies for commercial use, which necessitates protecting the incumbents while allowing other users to utilize the spectrum. An important precursor to that effort is the ability to accurately measure the spectrum with a high-accuracy and in a cost-effective manner is extremely important. Furthermore, enabling this sensing in near real-time could enable the FCC to enforce the policies in real-time and network operators to optimize the network. Specifically, we are close to achieving Shannon capacity at the physical layer. However, when it comes to managing the medium access control, our network throughput drops to less than 10 percent performance compared to what the PHY layer can promise. The challenge with traditional networking is it operates in isolation, i.e., it is only aware of the localized behavior of spectrum and network usage. The primary reason for such unawareness is the inability to monitor the spectrum with high-accuracy and make decisions with global knowledge instead of local knowledge.

  1. How can we help wireless networks deliver for the next decade of commercial and DoD applications?
  2. How can we help Science and DoD and other applications (GNSS, Radio-astronomy) to achieve interference-free periods to conduct sensing using spectrum?

The goal of the workshop is to identify key thrusts in achieving this vision via lightning talks and breakout sessions and discussion.

Aleks/Aleksandar Damnjanovic

Role of synchronization for efficient use of shared spectrum

The need for spectrum sharing among multiple technologies is likely to increase in the future. Database coexistence methods rely on the knowledge of the location, and the channel models applicable for a given deployment, to estimate path loss between the interfering nodes. For that reason, database methods need to be conservative to minimize interference among nodes sharing the spectrum. One advantage of the sensing based coexistence is that sensing can enable more accurate estimate of the path loss between the interfering nodes, and therefore, provides potential for more efficient spectrum sharing. Challenges arise in multi-technology deployments when transmissions are uncoordinated and it is difficult to estimate the path loss towards a “victim” receiver. Synchronization offers an opportunity to improve effectiveness of the sensing approach because it becomes feasible to motivate coordination of transmissions, which enables detection of signals at low energy levels and leads to efficient spectrum sharing while maintaining user service quality.

Ali Khayrallah

Notes on spectrum sharing

I will argue that the essence of spectrum sharing is coordination. Sharing occurs even in exclusive licensed spectrum as a network shares resources within itself. With multiple networks sharing the same spectrum, the degree and nature of coordination determine performance in the big picture. In particular, quality of service is a crucial issue that needs to be addressed to enable commercial success.

Mark McHenry

Spectrum Sharing Approaches

This talk addresses different spectrum sharing architectures. A key spectrum-sharing factor is propagation

loss uncertainty and how this affects the amount of spectrum available to entrant users. Local spectrum

sensing sharing architectures overcome this issue, but the local sensing approach doesn’t work in all

scenarios. Hence, multiple spectrum sharing rules are needed. Another important factor are complete and

accurate frequency assignment databases. The present frequency assignment databases have many issues,

which are a major impediment to all spectrum sharing architectures.

Rakesh Misra

Ushering in an era of programmability and intelligence in cellular radio access networks

The cellular radio access network is increasingly becoming the ubiquitous medium for last-mile connectivity, and is expanding beyond licensed spectrum to unlicensed/shared spectrum. Traditionally, cellular RANs have been closed systems with very limited programmability, which among other things has led to limited intelligence and efficiency of co-existence with other wireless systems. This talk will describe some of the recent advances in the industry to make the cellular RAN more open, programmable and intelligent. The talk will also explain the work that the speaker’s team at VMware is doing to usher in an era of programmability and intelligence in cellular RAN. Among other things, this capability presents a new opportunity for enabling intelligent and efficient co-existence of cellular RAN, and dynamic sharing of spectrum, with other wireless systems.

Rakesh Misra is a Director of R&D in VMware’s Telco & Edge Cloud Business Unit. For almost a decade, he has been on a mission to make the cellular radio access network (RAN) more programmable and intelligent, and to that end, has been driving R&D efforts in both academia and industry. Prior to joining VMware, he co-founded Uhana Inc. in 2015-16 to commercialize his Stanford research on data-driven mobile networking; Uhana Inc. was acquired by VMware in 2019. Now at VMware, his team is building a RAN controller platform to foster intelligent control-plane applications for the cellular RAN – this platform abstracts the underlying RAN infrastructure and provides easy-to-use APIs for application developers to monitor and control the RAN. He has a PhD from Stanford University, and BTech and MTech from Indian Institute of Technology Madras.

Raymond Shen, Gennady Farber

Spectrum coexistence and spectrum monitoring in the times of shared spectrum.

This talk reviews current methods of visualizing spectrum interference and spectrum coexistence. Using traditional and enhanced spectrum analysis methods we show how poorly managed spectrum can affect performance of communication links, either cellular, or satcom. Methods for extracting signal quality indicators vary between physical layer and communications layer, and we discuss tools to implement those measurements. When it comes to spectrum monitoring, then field deployable real-time methods and instruments come into the play. Instant processing of spectrum data for purposes of dynamic spectrum sharing and data-base based long term spectrum utilization are discussed. All this in 15 minutes.

Raymond Shen, PhD, directs Spectrum Monitoring and Field Test Solutions at Keysight Technologies. In this role he leads Keysight's spectrum monitoring group, managing a vast portfolio of solutions that include handheld, outdoor, and modular platforms. He has over 20 years of experience in spectrum monitoring and signal demodulation, with expertise in cellular signals. Shen has a BS EE from Caltech and a MS EE and PhD EE from Stanford University, where his PhD was in the area of neural networks. Gennady Farber responsibilities are developing test and performance validation solutions for Spectrum monitoring and related needs using Keysight family of products, with focus on testing in the spectrum domain. Gennady has an MS in Radio Physics from Leningrad Polytechnic Institute, Soviet Union.
